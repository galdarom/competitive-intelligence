Delta Analysis (vs. Prior Period)

What Accelerated

ARR Growth Re-Accelerates to +23%

Annual Recurring Revenue grew 23% in FY2025 to $23.9M, up from a 16% growth rate in the prior year. Order intake +20% to $33.8M. This is the second consecutive year of double-digit ARR growth, reinforcing the subscription-model thesis. Customer retention increased to 98% (from 97% in FY2024). Indicates Corero is converting trial deployments into multi-year recurring contracts more effectively.

Encrypted DDoS Capability (No Decryption)

October 2025 launch of SmartWall ONE encrypted application-layer protection. AI-assisted detection without traffic decryption (per Corero claim) addresses the same enterprise pain point Radware targets with its no-decrypt L7 DDoS protection. CEO claim of zero added latency and no infrastructure expansion. Two specialist vendors now positioning against Cloudflare/Akamai requirement to decrypt for L7 protection.

Channel Reach via HPE-Juniper Acquisition

HPE’s July 2025 close of Juniper acquisition extended Corero’s Juniper-router-integrated DDoS solution into HPE’s broader enterprise security channel. The April 2025 expanded partnership now flows through HPE-Juniper sales motion. Significantly larger addressable channel than Corero could build organically — effectively a force-multiplier for the SmartWall TDD line.

What Is New

SmartWall ONE 14.1 Adaptive Defense Platform

November 2025 enterprise datasheet release. Modular architecture scales 80–800 Gbps per appliance with no forklift upgrades. Sub-second inline mitigation across L3–7. CORE network intelligence integration provides traffic visibility alongside automated mitigation. Positioned for enterprise + service-provider as “Adaptive Defense Platform for Enterprise Resilience.”

Subscription / DDoSPaaS Pivot Becomes Visible

Reported “growing adoption of subscription-based and DDoS Protection-as-a-Service offerings” as a stated FY2025 trend. ARR growth (+23%) outpacing total revenue indicates the model transition is real, not aspirational. This shifts Corero’s revenue profile from one-time hardware sales to recurring services — the same business-model evolution Radware completed several years ago.

What Slowed or Faces Risk

Absolute Revenue Scale Remains Small

$24.6M FY2024 revenue and $23.9M FY2025 ARR keep Corero an order of magnitude below F5 ($3.1B), NETSCOUT cybersecurity (~$305M annualized), A10 ($291M), and Radware ($302M). Sub-$100M scale limits R&D investment, channel coverage, and analyst-relations spend. Re-rating thesis depends on sustained 20%+ ARR growth converting to credible scale within 3–5 years.

No WAAP / API Security / Bot Management

Corero is the most pure-play DDoS specialist in the comparison set. No WAF, no bot management, no API security, no ADC. Like NETSCOUT, this limits relevance in enterprise security consolidation cycles where buyers prefer platform vendors. Differentiation versus NETSCOUT: Corero is appliance-and-software-focused at smaller scale, NETSCOUT is appliance + carrier-scale + ATLAS telemetry.

Hyperscaler/Cloud-Native DDoS Pressure

Cloudflare 500 Tbps unmetered, Akamai Prolexic, AWS Shield Advanced, and Radware Cloud DDoS all bundle DDoS protection in larger platforms. Corero’s service-provider/MSP focus is partially insulated, but enterprise-direct deals face the same platform-bundling pressure as NETSCOUT. Mitigation: lean harder on the “DDoS specialist” identity and channel partnerships (HPE-Juniper, Akamai integration, GTT).

Meaningful Pattern Shifts

Two DDoS Specialists Converging on No-Decrypt L7

Both Corero (Oct 2025) and Radware (Mar 2026) launched encrypted L7 DDoS protection without traffic decryption within a 5-month window. This is a market signal: certificate-sensitive enterprises are demanding L7 DDoS that doesn’t require SSL key sharing. Cloudflare and Akamai do not currently match. The two specialists are independently arriving at the same architectural answer.

Channel-First DDoS Distribution Becomes Viable

Corero’s strategy — sell through HPE-Juniper, Akamai, GTT, and large MSPs rather than direct enterprise — demonstrates a viable smaller-vendor playbook in a market increasingly dominated by platform giants. ARR growth of 23% on $61M market cap indicates the channel-first model can work at sub-scale. Implication for Radware: channel partnerships in carrier/MSP segments may be more durable than direct-enterprise DDoS sales.

Strategic Signals

Signal 1: Two Specialists Independently Arrive at “No-Decrypt L7 DDoS”

Corero (Oct 2025) and Radware (Mar 2026) both launched encrypted L7 DDoS protection that does not require SSL key sharing or traffic decryption, within a 5-month window. This is convergent evolution — two independent specialists answering the same enterprise demand for certificate-sensitive DDoS protection. Cloudflare and Akamai do not currently match. The market signal: certificate control is a real buyer criterion in regulated industries, and specialists are out-innovating platforms on this dimension. For Radware, this is corroborating signal that the encrypted-DDoS angle has legs — and that Corero is the second voice in the same conversation.

Signal 2: ARR Growth Outpacing Revenue Validates the Subscription Pivot

Corero’s ARR grew 23% to $23.9M in FY2025 while order intake grew 20% to $33.8M. ARR growth ahead of order intake means existing customers are renewing at higher value (98% retention) and contracted future revenue is building. This is the same recurring-revenue transition Radware completed several years ago. Corero is replicating the playbook at smaller scale: from one-time appliance sales to subscription / DDoSPaaS. The pivot signal is real, not aspirational — the ARR/revenue ratio is rising.

Signal 3: HPE-Juniper Channel Activation Is Corero’s Force Multiplier

HPE’s July 2025 close of the Juniper acquisition extended Corero’s router-integrated DDoS solution into HPE’s entire enterprise security channel. For a $61M-market-cap specialist, suddenly being a security-component inside HPE’s sales motion is a force multiplier no organic GTM investment could match. The Juniper MX/PTX router-edge DDoS approach is also architecturally distinctive — no other Track 2 vendor places DDoS detection and line-rate mitigation directly in the router OS. For Radware, this means Corero will increasingly appear in HPE-led enterprise opportunities; competitive positioning needs to anticipate the joint solution rather than treat Corero as a standalone.

Signal 4: Channel-Led Distribution Is a Viable Sub-Scale DDoS Strategy

Corero’s strategic alliances list — HPE+Juniper, Akamai, GTT — demonstrates that a $24.6M-revenue DDoS specialist can establish global presence by riding larger partners’ channels. ARR growth of 23% on $61.5M market cap proves the channel-first model can produce double-digit growth without platform-vendor scale. For Radware, this is competitive intelligence about how smaller competitors will operate: not by out-spending, but by embedding into channel partners. The defensive implication: ensure Radware is the preferred DDoS specialist within HPE-Juniper, Akamai, and major MSP relationships before Corero entrenches further.

Signal 5: Two Specialists Differentiated by Telemetry Depth, Not Capability

Corero and NETSCOUT are both pure-play DDoS specialists, but their moats differ. NETSCOUT’s ATLAS monitors 400+ Tbps across 214 countries — a telemetry footprint Corero cannot match at $24.6M revenue. Corero’s differentiation has to come from product (modular SmartWall, no-decrypt encrypted DDoS, Juniper integration) and channel (HPE-Juniper, Akamai), not from telemetry. This makes Corero structurally more comparable to Radware (product-and-deployment-led) than to NETSCOUT (telemetry-and-research-led). Radware’s ERT and Global Threat Report sit between the two on the intelligence axis.

Signal 6: Corero’s Pure-Play Identity Mirrors NETSCOUT’s Limitations — and Radware’s Opportunity

Like NETSCOUT, Corero has no WAF, no bot management, no API security, no ADC. It competes exclusively on DDoS. Radware’s unified WAAP + DDoS + API security positioning is structurally stronger than Corero’s in enterprise security consolidation cycles, where buyers prefer fewer vendors. But in DDoS-specific evaluations — especially encrypted-DDoS / certificate-sensitive deals — Corero is a credible alternative. The competitive implication: Radware should target consolidation deals where DDoS is bundled with WAAP/API; concede pure-DDoS deals to specialists where Radware’s platform value isn’t the deciding factor.

Executive Takeaways

1

Corero is the second specialist independently endorsing the “encrypted DDoS without decryption” thesis

Corero’s October 2025 SmartWall ONE encrypted DDoS launch arrived 5 months before Radware’s March 2026 announcement. Two independent DDoS specialists landing on the same architectural answer is corroborating evidence that no-decrypt L7 protection is a real enterprise demand. Radware should expect Corero to be a co-claimant in pitches focused on certificate-sensitive verticals (finance, healthcare, government).

2

Corero is sub-scale today but growing ARR faster than any peer in the comparison set

$24.6M revenue vs. F5 ($3.1B) or NETSCOUT ($823M) makes Corero a marginal financial threat in absolute terms. But ARR growth of +23% YoY exceeds the growth rate of every peer. If sustained for 3–5 years — a non-trivial “if” given absolute scale — Corero would approach A10/Radware revenue territory. Radware should monitor as a competitor on capability today and on scale in 2028–2029.

3

HPE-Juniper integration changes how Radware will encounter Corero in deals

Corero’s SmartWall TDD is integrated into Juniper MX/PTX routers and now part of HPE’s enterprise security channel. Going forward, Corero will increasingly appear inside HPE-led opportunities rather than as a standalone. Competitive positioning needs to anticipate the joint HPE-Juniper-Corero offering — not just Corero in isolation. Radware should prioritize being the preferred DDoS specialist in HPE-Juniper, Akamai, and major MSP partner relationships.

4

Radware’s WAAP + DDoS + API security platform is structurally stronger than Corero’s pure-play offering in consolidation deals

Like NETSCOUT, Corero has no WAF, no bot management, no API security. In enterprise security consolidation cycles, Radware’s unified platform competes on more than DDoS depth alone. The competitive playbook: target opportunities where DDoS is bundled with WAAP/API decisions; concede pure-DDoS bake-offs in regulated industries where capability depth and certificate handling are the only criteria.

5

Channel-led DDoS distribution is the smaller-vendor playbook Radware should learn from

Corero’s GTM strategy — sell through HPE-Juniper, Akamai, GTT, MSPs — produced 23% ARR growth at $61M market cap with limited direct sales motion. For Radware, this validates investing in carrier/MSP partnership depth as a more durable channel than direct enterprise DDoS sales, which face Cloudflare/Akamai platform-bundling pressure. Implication: prioritize being indispensable to a small number of large channels.

6

The Track 2 specialist field is now four vendors — with Corero as the smallest, most innovation-rich, and channel-dependent

Track 2 (DDoS Appliance Vendors) now includes F5, NETSCOUT Arbor, A10, and Corero. The four vendors differentiate by scale, telemetry, integration, and channel rather than by feature set — all offer similar DDoS capability fundamentals. Corero’s unique fit: smallest revenue, highest ARR growth, deepest router integration (Juniper), and most channel-dependent GTM. Radware should treat Corero as a credible specialist competitor in DDoS-specific deals and as a partner-channel competitor in HPE-Juniper opportunities.

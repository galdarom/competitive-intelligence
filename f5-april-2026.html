Delta Analysis (vs. Prior Period)

What Accelerated

Product Revenue Momentum Is Exceptional

22% product revenue growth in Q2 FY2026 marks the 7th consecutive quarter of double-digit product growth. This isn’t a one-quarter blip — it reflects sustained enterprise demand for BIG-IP, NGINX, and Distributed Cloud. Guidance raised to 7–8% total growth (from 5–6%), signaling confidence in the pipeline.

AI Demand Is Driving Real Revenue

AI bookings hit $50M in H1 FY2026, up 200% YoY. AI inference workloads require application delivery and security at the network edge — F5’s core competency. The convergence of AI traffic patterns with multicloud networking creates a natural growth vector for F5’s platform.

Software/SaaS Transformation Continues

F5’s shift from hardware to software/SaaS is maturing. The ADSP Partner Program and Distributed Cloud Services expansion signal that the platform-first strategy is taking hold. Annual revenue surpassed $3B and operating profit exceeded $1B for the first time in FY2025.

What Is New

ADSP Ecosystem with Proactive DDoS Testing

The MazeBolt integration within the ADSP Partner Program is notable: it enables proactive, automated DDoS vulnerability assessment before attacks occur. This shifts the DDoS value proposition from reactive mitigation to continuous validation — a capability neither NETSCOUT nor A10 currently offers through partnerships.

Chairman + CEO Consolidation

Locoh-Donou adding Chairman role signals board alignment behind the multicloud transformation. This governance consolidation typically precedes major strategic moves (M&A, platform shifts, market expansion).

What Slowed or Faces Risk

CISA Emergency Directive: Nation-State Breach of F5 Infrastructure

ED 26-01 revealed that a nation-state actor had persistent access to F5’s BIG-IP development environment. CVE-2025-53521 (CVSS 9.8) is actively exploited. This is a severe trust event — the DDoS protection infrastructure itself was compromised. Federal agencies ordered to inventory and patch all F5 devices. For competitors including Radware, this creates a selling opportunity in federal and regulated enterprise accounts.

DDoS Is Not F5’s Core Identity

F5’s $3.1B revenue spans application delivery, WAF, bot management, API security, load balancing, and DDoS. DDoS is one product line among many. Distributed Cloud DDoS Mitigation at 12+ Tbps is adequate but not market-leading vs. Cloudflare (500 Tbps), Akamai Prolexic (20+ Tbps), or NETSCOUT Arbor (15+ Tbps dedicated). F5 competes on breadth, not DDoS depth.

Meaningful Pattern Shifts

DDoS Becomes Table Stakes in the Multicloud Platform Sale

F5’s strategy positions DDoS as one component of a broader Application Delivery and Security Platform (ADSP). This mirrors Cloudflare’s approach — DDoS bundled into a platform sale, not sold as a standalone service. For DDoS specialists like NETSCOUT, A10, and Radware, this platformization trend compresses the standalone DDoS market.

Security Infrastructure Itself Is Now a Target

The F5 BIG-IP breach + CISA directive demonstrates that DDoS/security appliances are high-value nation-state targets. Organizations must now secure their security infrastructure — a recursive problem that favors cloud-native architectures (no on-prem appliance to breach) and vendors with strong supply-chain security postures.

Strategic Signals

Signal 1: F5’s CISA Breach Creates a Window of Vulnerability in Federal and Regulated Markets

The nation-state breach of F5’s BIG-IP development environment (ED 26-01) and the critical CVE-2025-53521 RCE vulnerability (CVSS 9.8, actively exploited) represent the most significant security incident among DDoS vendors in this period. Federal agencies and regulated enterprises must now reassess the security of their F5 infrastructure itself — creating a displacement opportunity for Radware, NETSCOUT, and A10 in accounts where F5 trust has been damaged. This is particularly relevant for on-prem BIG-IP DDoS Hybrid Defender deployments where the compromised appliance is the last line of defense.

Signal 2: F5 Is Platformizing DDoS — Which Compresses the Standalone DDoS Market

F5’s ADSP strategy bundles DDoS with WAF, bot management, API security, and application delivery in a single platform. At $3.1B revenue and $17.2B market cap, F5 can afford to undervalue DDoS as a standalone line item. For Radware, NETSCOUT, and A10, F5’s platformization mirrors the Cloudflare/Akamai threat in the WAAP market: DDoS becomes “included” rather than “purchased.” The strategic response for DDoS specialists is to compete on depth, intelligence, and specialization that platforms cannot match.

Signal 3: AI Infrastructure Demand Benefits All DDoS Vendors — But Differently

F5’s $50M AI bookings, A10’s AI infrastructure contract win, and the broader trend of AI inference at the edge all increase the attack surface for DDoS. AI workloads are latency-sensitive and high-value — making them premium DDoS targets. F5 and A10 are positioning to protect AI infrastructure directly. NETSCOUT’s ATLAS telemetry detects AI-augmented attack patterns. Radware’s Agentic AI Protection addresses the AI-agent-specific threat. Each vendor captures a different slice of the AI-DDoS opportunity.

Signal 4: Proactive DDoS Testing Is an Emerging Differentiator

F5’s MazeBolt integration within ADSP enables automated DDoS vulnerability assessment before attacks occur. This shifts the value proposition from “we mitigate when attacked” to “we prevent successful attacks proactively.” No other DDoS vendor in this comparison offers an equivalent proactive testing capability as an integrated platform feature. Radware should evaluate whether a similar proactive DDoS testing partnership or feature would strengthen its competitive positioning.

Signal 5: F5’s Revenue Scale Creates Pricing Power That Specialists Cannot Match

At $3.1B total revenue, F5 can price DDoS aggressively as a loss leader within platform deals. When F5 competes against Radware in an enterprise account, DDoS pricing may be artificially low because F5 recovers margin on ADC, WAF, and other services. Radware’s counter-positioning must emphasize DDoS depth, encrypted attack handling, and hybrid flexibility that justify standalone pricing against bundled platform offerings.

Signal 6: The Hybrid Multicloud Trend Favors Vendors with Both On-Prem and Cloud

90%+ enterprises operate across an average of 19 locations (per F5). This hybrid reality means DDoS protection must span on-prem, private cloud, and public cloud. All four vendors in this comparison offer hybrid DDoS — but F5 and Radware are best positioned because both have strong on-prem appliances AND cloud services. NETSCOUT leads in ISP/carrier but is less present in public cloud. A10 excels in telco but is building enterprise cloud presence.

Executive Takeaways

1

F5 is the largest and best-funded competitor in the Cloud DDoS market — but DDoS is not its primary focus

At $3.1B revenue and $17.2B market cap, F5 has more resources than NETSCOUT, A10, and Radware combined. But F5’s strategic attention is on multicloud application delivery, AI inference, and platform breadth. DDoS is one feature among many. This means F5 invests less in DDoS-specific innovation than dedicated players, but can bundle DDoS at prices specialists cannot match.

2

The CISA BIG-IP breach is Radware’s strongest competitive lever against F5 in 2026

ED 26-01 and CVE-2025-53521 expose F5’s on-prem infrastructure as a nation-state target. In federal, government, and regulated enterprise accounts, Radware should directly address this trust gap. Radware’s DefensePro has not faced a comparable supply-chain security incident.

3

F5’s platformization of DDoS mirrors the Cloudflare/Akamai dynamic in WAAP

ADSP bundles DDoS with WAF, bot, API security, and ADC. This makes standalone DDoS harder to sell against F5 in accounts that want platform consolidation. Radware’s unified WAAP + DDoS + API security positioning competes better than NETSCOUT (DDoS-only) or A10 (DDoS + ADC) against F5’s bundled approach.

4

In cloud DDoS scrubbing, F5 (12+ Tbps) is comparable to NETSCOUT (15+ Tbps) but lags on threat intelligence

F5’s Distributed Cloud offers 12+ Tbps across 24 Regional Edges — adequate but not market-leading. F5 lacks the equivalent of NETSCOUT’s ATLAS (800+ Tbps visibility, 65+ threat actor tracking). Radware should position its threat intelligence and ERT capabilities as the differentiator when both F5 and Radware are in the same deal.

5

F5’s AI bookings ($50M, +200%) signal that AI infrastructure protection will be a growth battleground

AI workloads are DDoS targets. F5 is capturing this demand through its multicloud platform. Radware should articulate its AI-specific DDoS protection story (Agentic AI Protection + DefensePro behavioral ML for AI inference traffic) to compete for AI infrastructure security budgets.

6

Radware’s encrypted DDoS capability is its strongest differentiator against all three competitors

Neither F5, NETSCOUT, nor A10 offers encrypted L7 DDoS mitigation without decryption (per Radware claim). In environments where TLS certificate sharing is unacceptable (financial services, healthcare, government), Radware’s DefensePro addresses a use case that no competitor can. This is the single most defensible technical differentiator across the Cloud DDoS Services market.

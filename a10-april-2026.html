Delta Analysis (vs. Prior Period)

What Accelerated

Revenue Growth Is Accelerating — Record After Record

FY2025 was A10’s record year ($290.6M, +11%), followed by a record Q4 ($80.4M). Q1 2026 continued the trend at $75.0M (+13.4%), beating estimates. Three of the last four quarters showed double-digit growth. The 2026 guide of 10–12% growth with 28–30% EBITDA margins signals sustainable, profitable acceleration — not one-time wins.

AI Infrastructure Is Becoming a Core Growth Driver

The Microsoft AI infrastructure win validates A10’s positioning. AI workloads require high-performance DDoS protection at network ingress — exactly where Thunder TPS operates. CEO Trivedi explicitly linked demand to AI buildouts and 5G rollouts. A10 is converting “AI hype” into signed contracts with tier-1 cloud providers.

Stock Performance Reflects Market Confidence

ATEN is up 84% in 12 months, reaching its 52-week high near $28.59. Market cap approaching $2B. This outperforms the broader cybersecurity sector and reflects investor confidence in A10’s growth trajectory, cash generation ($369.8M cash, zero debt), and consistent earnings beats.

What Is New

ThreatX Acquisition Extends A10 Beyond DDoS into WAAP

The ThreatX Protect acquisition (Feb 2025) adds API protection, bot management, and next-gen WAF to A10 Defend. This is a strategic pivot: A10 is no longer a DDoS/ADC specialist but a hybrid security platform covering DDoS + application security. This directly overlaps with Radware’s WAAP + DDoS positioning and Radware’s own Pynt acquisition for API security.

Turkcell Partnership Expands Carrier Footprint

The Turkcell collaboration for network virtualization and carrier-grade NAT infrastructure reinforces A10’s telco dominance (9 of top 10 telcos). As 5G deployments accelerate, carrier-grade security becomes a growth vector. A10’s carrier relationships are deeper than any competitor in the DDoS comparison set.

What Slowed or Faces Risk

Cloud DDoS Scrubbing Capacity Remains Undisclosed

A10 does not publicly disclose its cloud DDoS scrubbing capacity in Tbps. NETSCOUT publishes 15+ Tbps, F5 publishes 12+ Tbps. This lack of transparency is a competitive liability in RFP processes where buyers compare cloud mitigation scale. A10’s on-prem strength (300 Gbps per appliance) doesn’t compensate for cloud ambiguity.

Scale Remains a Constraint vs. F5 and Platform Players

At $290.6M revenue, A10 is ~10.6x smaller than F5 ($3.1B) and ~2.8x smaller than NETSCOUT ($823M). While A10 grows faster, its absolute scale limits R&D investment, go-to-market reach, and pricing power against larger competitors who can bundle DDoS as a platform feature. Radware ($301.9M) is the most directly comparable competitor by revenue scale.

Meaningful Pattern Shifts

DDoS Specialists Are Becoming Security Platforms

A10’s ThreatX acquisition mirrors Radware’s Pynt acquisition: both DDoS specialists are expanding into WAAP/API security. Meanwhile, platform players (F5, Cloudflare, Akamai) are absorbing DDoS into broader offerings. The standalone DDoS market is compressing from both directions — specialists expanding up and platforms absorbing down. The winners will be those who combine DDoS depth with platform breadth credibly.

Carrier/Telco Channel Is A10’s Moat

With 9 of the top 10 telcos and 8 of the top 10 cloud providers as customers, A10’s carrier channel is its deepest competitive moat. The Turkcell expansion and 5G infrastructure demand create a renewal-driven, high-retention revenue base (90%+ renewal rates). Neither Radware nor F5 has comparable carrier penetration. NETSCOUT competes in this channel via ISP-grade Sightline/TMS but with a different deployment model.

Strategic Signals

Signal 1: A10 Is Radware’s Most Direct Competitor — Similar Scale, Overlapping Strategy

At $290.6M vs. Radware’s $301.9M, A10 is the closest revenue peer in the DDoS market. Both companies are pursuing the same strategic pivot: expanding from DDoS into WAAP/API security (A10 via ThreatX, Radware via Pynt). Both target hybrid enterprise and carrier environments. Both have behavioral/ML DDoS detection. A10 is the competitor most likely to appear in the same deals as Radware — every feature comparison, pricing benchmark, and win/loss should track A10 as the primary peer threat.

Signal 2: A10’s Carrier/Telco Moat Is Deep and Expanding

9 of the top 10 telcos and 8 of the top 10 cloud providers. The Turkcell expansion, 5G infrastructure demand, and carrier-grade NAT deployments create a customer base that is sticky and high-value. Radware competes for telco DDoS deals, but A10’s carrier penetration is deeper. In tier-1 telco accounts, A10 is the incumbent to displace — Radware should focus on mid-tier carriers and enterprise accounts where A10’s carrier brand is less dominant.

Signal 3: The Microsoft AI Win Signals A10’s Credibility in Hyperscale Environments

Being selected by Microsoft for AI infrastructure protection is a significant reference win. It validates that Thunder TPS’s 300 Gbps per-appliance performance meets hyperscale requirements. If A10 can replicate this across other hyperscalers, it establishes a beachhead in AI infrastructure security that Radware currently lacks. Radware should develop and publicize its own AI infrastructure protection reference architectures before A10 builds a case study portfolio.

Signal 4: A10’s ThreatX Acquisition Creates Direct WAAP Overlap with Radware

ThreatX Protect adds API protection, bot management, and next-gen WAF to A10 Defend. This mirrors Radware’s Pynt acquisition for API security. A10 and Radware are converging on the same “DDoS + WAAP” hybrid security platform positioning. In competitive evaluations, A10 can now claim both DDoS and application security — a capability that was previously a Radware differentiator against A10. Radware’s encrypted DDoS mitigation and Agentic AI Protection remain unique differentiators.

Signal 5: A10’s Financial Health Creates Acquisition or Sustained Growth Optionality

$369.8M cash, zero debt, 30% EBITDA margins, and record bookings give A10 strategic flexibility. A10 could use its cash for additional acquisitions (expanding the Defend portfolio), aggressive pricing to win deals, or share buybacks to boost stock price. Radware should monitor A10’s M&A activity closely — the next acquisition will signal whether A10 is building a platform (threat to Radware) or staying focused on DDoS/ADC (less direct overlap).

Signal 6: A10’s Undisclosed Cloud Capacity Is Its Biggest Competitive Vulnerability

In an era where NETSCOUT publishes 15+ Tbps and F5 publishes 12+ Tbps, A10’s refusal to disclose cloud DDoS capacity creates uncertainty. In RFPs where cloud mitigation scale is a qualifying criterion, A10’s ambiguity is an exploitable weakness for Radware. Radware sales teams should explicitly ask prospects whether A10 has disclosed cloud scrubbing capacity — the answer will consistently be “no,” which raises questions about A10’s cloud readiness in volumetric attack scenarios.

Executive Takeaways

1

A10 is Radware’s closest peer competitor — same scale, converging strategy, overlapping accounts

At $290.6M vs. $301.9M, A10 and Radware are within 4% of each other on revenue. Both are pivoting from DDoS to hybrid DDoS + WAAP platforms (ThreatX vs. Pynt). Both target enterprise and carrier markets. A10 should be treated as Radware’s primary head-to-head competitor, ahead of F5 (too large/broad) and NETSCOUT (different deployment model).

2

A10’s on-prem DDoS performance is market-leading and a direct threat in high-throughput accounts

Thunder TPS at 300 Gbps / 440 Mpps per appliance is high performance with NimbusDDOS certification — though Radware DefensePro reaches 800 Gbps per appliance at the high end (the highest in the comparison set). A10’s strength is scale-out clustering (2.4 Tbps cluster) and extreme packets-per-second (440 Mpps). In telco, data center, and AI infrastructure accounts where per-rack throughput plus cluster scaling matter, A10 is competitive. Radware competes on detection intelligence, encrypted DDoS, hybrid flexibility, and per-appliance throughput at the very high end.

3

A10’s carrier/telco dominance (9/10 top telcos) is a moat Radware cannot easily breach

A10’s tier-1 carrier relationships are entrenched through ADC, DDoS, and CGNAT deployments. The Turkcell expansion and 5G demand reinforce this. Radware should focus competitive efforts on enterprise, mid-tier carriers, and regulated verticals where A10’s carrier brand carries less weight.

4

A10’s undisclosed cloud DDoS capacity is Radware’s most exploitable competitive lever

While NETSCOUT publishes 15+ Tbps and F5 publishes 12+ Tbps, A10 does not disclose its cloud scrubbing capacity. In cloud-first or hybrid DDoS evaluations, this is a qualifying weakness. Radware should ensure RFP responses explicitly ask for cloud capacity disclosure and position this gap prominently in competitive battlecards.

5

Radware’s encrypted DDoS mitigation remains the strongest technical differentiator against A10

A10’s Thunder TPS requires SSL decryption for L7 DDoS inspection. Radware’s DefensePro mitigates encrypted L7 DDoS without decryption (per Radware claim). In environments where TLS certificate sharing is unacceptable (financial services, healthcare, government), this is a decisive capability gap in Radware’s favor.

6

Monitor A10’s next acquisition closely — it will signal the competitive trajectory

With $369.8M cash and zero debt, A10 has significant M&A firepower. ThreatX was the first move into WAAP. If A10 acquires a cloud DDoS scrubbing provider or a threat intelligence company, it would close its two biggest competitive gaps. Radware should prepare competitive positioning for an “A10 with cloud scale” scenario — because A10 can afford to buy what it doesn’t have.
